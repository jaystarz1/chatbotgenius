Conventional wisdom suggests sophisticated corporate lawyers would embrace AI while solo practitioners resist. The data reveals the opposite. They're not looking for moonshot AI capabilities‚Äîthey want practical tools that slot into their existing processes.

This pragmatic approach sidesteps the paralysis plaguing larger firms. While BigLaw worries about reputational risk from AI hallucinations (remember the Mata v. Yet personal injury lawyers navigate these concerns through selective adoption. They're not using AI to argue constitutional law or craft Supreme Court briefs. They're using it for document analysis, routine drafting, and administrative tasks where verification is straightforward. As one survey respondent noted, PI firms are more likely than others to expect AI to replace outsourced work (19% vs. 12% overall)‚Äînot because they're reckless, but because they understand exactly which tasks AI handles reliably.
