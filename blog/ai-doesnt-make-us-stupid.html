The Economist recently published an article suggesting that AI is making us less intelligent, painting a picture of cognitive decline as we rely more on artificial intelligence tools. I fundamentally disagree with this premise. As someone who works with AI daily and teaches others how to leverage these tools effectively, I've seen firsthand how AI enhances rather than diminishes human cognitive abilities.

The Flawed Premise of AI-Induced Stupidity

The argument that AI makes us stupid rests on a fundamental misunderstanding of how humans interact with tools. It's like saying calculators made us bad at math or GPS made us unable to navigate. While it's true that we might not memorize as many phone numbers or multiplication tables as we once did, this doesn't represent a loss of intelligence‚Äîit represents a reallocation of cognitive resources.

When I use ChatGPT or Claude to help me write, research, or solve problems, I'm not becoming less intelligent. I'm becoming more efficient. The time and mental energy I save on routine tasks gets redirected to higher-level thinking, creative problem-solving, and strategic planning.

Critics often confuse dependency with stupidity. Yes, I depend on AI tools, just as I depend on my glasses to see clearly. Neither makes me less capable‚Äîthey both enhance my natural abilities.

AI as a Cognitive Enhancement Tool

Think of AI as a cognitive exoskeleton. Just as a physical exoskeleton doesn't make your muscles weaker (it allows you to lift more), AI doesn't make your brain weaker‚Äîit allows you to think bigger.

Here's what AI actually does for our intelligence:

  • Amplifies research capabilities: I can explore topics in depth that would have taken weeks to research manually
  • Enhances creativity: By handling routine tasks, AI frees my mind for creative thinking
  • Improves learning speed: I can quickly understand complex topics by asking AI to explain them in different ways
  • Enables better decision-making: Access to instant analysis helps me make more informed choices

In my work as The Chatbot Genius, I've taught thousands of people how to use AI effectively. Not one of them has become "stupider"‚Äîthey've all become more capable and confident in tackling complex challenges.

Historical Parallels: Every Tool Has Its Critics

This isn't the first time we've heard these arguments. Throughout history, every major technological advancement has been met with concerns about human capability:

Writing: Socrates worried that writing would weaken memory (he was wrong‚Äîit allowed us to build upon previous knowledge)

Printing Press: Critics feared it would spread misinformation and make people lazy readers (instead, it democratized knowledge)

Calculators: Educators worried students would lose basic math skills (we now solve more complex problems than ever)

Internet: People said it would make us shallow thinkers (it connected global knowledge like never before)

Each time, the critics focused on what we might lose rather than what we would gain. The same is happening with AI.

The Real Benefits I See Daily

Let me share some concrete examples from my own experience and that of my clients:

Enhanced Productivity

A marketing manager I work with now creates comprehensive campaign strategies in hours instead of days. Is she less intelligent because AI helps her analyze market data? No‚Äîshe's able to focus on creative strategy while AI handles the data crunching.

Deeper Learning

Students I've trained use AI to explore topics from multiple angles, ask follow-up questions, and test their understanding. They're learning faster and more thoroughly than ever before.

Better Problem-Solving

Developers using AI coding assistants aren't becoming worse programmers‚Äîthey're solving more complex problems by delegating routine coding tasks to AI.

Addressing the Valid Concerns

I won't pretend there aren't legitimate concerns about AI's impact on human cognition. But these concerns should drive us to use AI more thoughtfully, not abandon it entirely.

The Risk of Mental Atrophy

Yes, if we completely outsource thinking to AI, our cognitive abilities could decline. But the same is true of any tool. The solution isn't to avoid AI‚Äîit's to use it as a complement to, not a replacement for, human thinking.

Over-Reliance on AI

Balance is key. I still do mental math, write first drafts by hand sometimes, and solve problems without AI assistance. These practices keep my cognitive abilities sharp while still benefiting from AI enhancement.

Critical Thinking Skills

AI makes critical thinking more important, not less. We need to evaluate AI outputs, recognize biases, and make informed decisions about when and how to use these tools.

The Future of Augmented Intelligence

The future isn't about humans versus AI‚Äîit's about humans with AI. This augmented intelligence will allow us to:

  • Solve previously unsolvable problems
  • Create at scales we never imagined
  • Learn and adapt faster than ever before
  • Focus on uniquely human skills like empathy, creativity, and strategic thinking

The people who thrive will be those who learn to work with AI, not those who resist it out of fear.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: But doesn't using AI for writing make us worse writers?

A: Not if used properly. AI can help with brainstorming, editing, and overcoming writer's block. The creativity, voice, and message still come from you. Think of it as having a writing assistant, not a replacement writer.

Q: What about students using AI to complete assignments?

A: This is about evolving education, not cheating. Students need to learn how to work with AI effectively‚Äîit's a crucial skill for their future careers. The solution is to update how we teach and assess, not ban the technology.

Q: Won't we lose the ability to think without AI?

A: Only if we choose to. Just as we still walk despite having cars, we can maintain our cognitive abilities while using AI. It's about augmentation, not replacement.

Q: How can I use AI without becoming dependent on it?

A: Set boundaries. Use AI for specific tasks, but regularly challenge yourself without it. Think of AI as one tool in your toolkit, not your only tool.

Conclusion: Embracing Cognitive Enhancement

AI doesn't make us stupid‚Äîavoiding AI in 2025 might. The key is learning to use these tools wisely, maintaining our core cognitive abilities while amplifying them with artificial intelligence.

The Economist's concerns reflect a common fear of change, but history shows us that each technological leap forward has ultimately enhanced human capability, not diminished it. AI is no different.

As we move forward, let's focus not on whether AI makes us stupid, but on how we can use it to become smarter, more creative, and more capable than ever before. The future belongs to those who learn to dance with AI, not those who sit out the dance.
